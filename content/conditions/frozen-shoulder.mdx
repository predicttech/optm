# Frozen Shoulder

Frozen Shoulder, medically known as **Adhesive Capsulitis**, is a condition that causes pain, stiffness, and a progressive loss of movement in the shoulder joint. Unlike a simple muscle strain, frozen shoulder develops gradually and can significantly limit your ability to perform everyday activities such as reaching overhead, dressing, driving, or sleeping comfortably.

The condition typically develops over several months and may take one to three years to fully recover if left untreated. Early diagnosis and appropriate treatment can help reduce pain, improve mobility, and shorten recovery time.

---

# What is Frozen Shoulder?

Frozen shoulder occurs when the connective tissue (capsule) surrounding the shoulder joint becomes inflamed, thickened, and tight. As the capsule tightens, scar tissue develops, restricting the shoulder's range of motion.

The condition usually affects only one shoulder, although it can occasionally develop in the opposite shoulder later.

Frozen shoulder progresses through three stages:

- Freezing Stage
- Frozen Stage
- Thawing Stage

---

# Stages of Frozen Shoulder

## 1. Freezing Stage

During this stage:

- Pain gradually increases
- Shoulder movement becomes difficult
- Night pain becomes common

This phase may last **2–9 months**.

---

## 2. Frozen Stage

Pain may begin to improve, but stiffness becomes much worse.

People often struggle with:

- Reaching overhead
- Wearing clothes
- Combing hair
- Driving

This stage usually lasts **4–12 months**.

---

## 3. Thawing Stage

The shoulder slowly regains mobility.

Recovery may take **6–24 months**, depending on treatment and individual factors.

---

# How Common is Frozen Shoulder?

Frozen shoulder affects approximately **2–5% of adults** and is most common in people between **40 and 60 years of age**.

It is more frequently seen in:

- Women
- People with diabetes
- Individuals recovering from shoulder injuries
- People who have undergone surgery requiring prolonged arm immobilization

---

# What Causes Frozen Shoulder?

The exact cause isn't always known, but several factors increase the risk.

## 1. Shoulder Immobilization

Keeping the shoulder still for weeks after:

- Surgery
- Fractures
- Rotator cuff injuries
- Arm injuries

can increase the risk.

---

## 2. Diabetes

People with diabetes are significantly more likely to develop frozen shoulder.

---

## 3. Previous Shoulder Injury

Trauma can trigger inflammation around the shoulder capsule.

---

## 4. Surgery

Shoulder, chest, or upper arm surgeries sometimes lead to reduced shoulder movement during recovery.

---

## 5. Muscle Weakness

Weak shoulder and upper back muscles reduce joint stability and contribute to movement limitations.

---

## 6. Age-Related Changes

The condition becomes more common as connective tissues naturally become less flexible with age.

---

# Symptoms of Frozen Shoulder

Symptoms develop gradually.

Common symptoms include:

- Shoulder pain
- Progressive stiffness
- Difficulty raising the arm
- Pain during movement
- Night pain
- Difficulty sleeping on the affected side
- Reduced range of motion
- Pain while reaching behind the back
- Difficulty dressing
- Difficulty combing hair

---

# Who is at Risk?

Your risk is higher if you:

- Are between 40 and 60 years old
- Have diabetes
- Recently had shoulder surgery
- Experienced a shoulder injury
- Have thyroid disorders
- Have Parkinson's disease
- Have cardiovascular disease
- Have prolonged shoulder immobilization

---

# At What Age Does Frozen Shoulder Occur?

### Young Adults (20–35)

Rare, usually after trauma or surgery.

### Middle Age (40–60)

Most common age group.

### Older Adults (60+)

May occur alongside arthritis or rotator cuff disease.

---

# When Should You See a Doctor?

Consult a healthcare professional if:

- Shoulder stiffness continues to worsen
- Pain lasts longer than a few weeks
- You cannot lift your arm
- Daily activities become difficult
- Night pain affects sleep
- Symptoms develop after surgery or injury

---

# How is Frozen Shoulder Diagnosed?

Diagnosis usually includes:

## Medical History

Your healthcare provider will ask about:

- Pain onset
- Previous injuries
- Medical conditions
- Daily limitations

---

## Physical Examination

Assessment includes:

- Active range of motion
- Passive range of motion
- Muscle strength
- Joint mobility

---

## Imaging Tests

Imaging may include:

- X-ray
- MRI
- Ultrasound

These tests help rule out other shoulder conditions.

---

# Treatment for Frozen Shoulder

Treatment focuses on reducing pain and restoring movement.

## Physical Therapy

Physical therapy is one of the most effective treatments.

It helps:

- Restore flexibility
- Improve shoulder mobility
- Reduce stiffness
- Strengthen surrounding muscles

---

## Exercise

Common exercises include:

- Pendulum exercises
- Shoulder stretches
- Wall climbing exercises
- Range-of-motion exercises

Exercises should be performed consistently and under professional guidance.

---

## Medications

Doctors may recommend:

- Pain relievers
- Anti-inflammatory medications

Medication should only be taken as prescribed.

---

## Corticosteroid Injections

In some cases, steroid injections help reduce inflammation and improve shoulder movement.

---

## Manipulation Under Anesthesia

In severe cases, doctors may gently move the shoulder while the patient is under anesthesia to improve mobility.

---

## Surgery

Arthroscopic surgery may be considered if symptoms persist despite several months of conservative treatment.

---

# Can Frozen Shoulder Be Prevented?

Although not all cases are preventable, you can reduce your risk by:

- Beginning shoulder movement early after injury (as advised by your healthcare provider)
- Performing regular shoulder mobility exercises
- Managing diabetes effectively
- Maintaining good posture
- Strengthening shoulder and upper back muscles

---

# Frequently Asked Questions

## Is frozen shoulder permanent?

No. Most people recover, although complete recovery may take several months to a few years.

---

## Does frozen shoulder go away on its own?

It may gradually improve over time, but treatment often helps reduce pain and restore movement more quickly.

---

## Is frozen shoulder the same as arthritis?

No. Arthritis involves joint degeneration, while frozen shoulder is caused by tightening and inflammation of the joint capsule.

---

## Can exercise help frozen shoulder?

Yes. Gentle stretching and physical therapy are key parts of treatment and recovery.

---

## Who is most likely to develop frozen shoulder?

People between 40 and 60 years old, especially women and individuals with diabetes, are at the highest risk.

---

# Take the First Step Toward Restoring Shoulder Mobility

Frozen shoulder can make even simple daily activities frustrating and painful. Early diagnosis and the right treatment plan can improve mobility, reduce pain, and help you regain normal shoulder function.

A comprehensive assessment can help identify the severity of your condition and guide you toward the most appropriate treatment.
